# Track C — Conceptual analysis: can any problem be translated into a QM picture?

**Status:** Companion to the Track C decision doc. The decision doc (`2026-06-17-track-c-decision.md`) records the *empirical* verdict from 3 trials: 0 of 3 load-bearing → deprecate. This doc records the *conceptual* answer to the harder question the user raised:

> "Can we fix it so any problem will be translated into a QM / GR problem?"

Short answer: **no, and the empirical failure is the symptom — not the cause.** A program that promises a universal translation is structurally untenable for the reasons below.

## What "translate a problem into a QM picture" must mean if it is to be load-bearing

A QM picture is a specific mathematical structure: a Hilbert space, observables as Hermitian operators, states as rays or density matrices, dynamics as unitary or completely-positive-trace-preserving maps, measurement as projective decomposition obeying the Born rule. To claim a translation is *load-bearing* (the C.2 criterion) is to claim an isomorphism or at least a useful homomorphism between this structure and the engineering problem at hand — a mapping that preserves enough structure to yield a falsifiable prediction the non-physics view would not have produced.

The conceptual question is whether such a mapping exists *for arbitrary engineering context*. The answer is "no" along several independent axes.

## The ten conceptual issues

### 1. No isomorphism is on offer; only vocabulary is borrowed

Most engineering problems lack the structural prerequisites: linear superposition with operationally meaningful interference, observables that genuinely fail to commute, dynamics generated by a Hermitian operator. A refactor decision does not superpose. A test suite is not Hermitian. Measuring code coverage does not collapse a wavefunction. Without the structural match, "translation" reduces to renaming — useful only if you wanted the new names.

### 2. The Born rule has no engineering correlate

The most distinctive QM feature is that outcome probabilities are squared magnitudes of complex amplitudes, with relative phases producing interference. For an engineering translation to be predictive (rather than merely descriptive), there must be: a complex amplitude attached to candidates, squared magnitudes that sum to one *and represent real outcome probabilities*, and phases that have operational meaning (because phases drive interference).

In software, what phase does "the probability we choose refactor option A" carry? Real probabilities suffice; introducing a complex phase adds a free parameter with no measurable consequence — under-determining the model rather than constraining it.

### 3. Genuine non-commutativity is rare

QM's signature surprises come from observables that fail to commute: measuring X disturbs P. For the picture to buy anything in engineering, you need pairs of measurements where one *physically* perturbs the other. Software measurements are usually static reads; reading coupling does not change test coverage. Without genuine non-commutativity, the uncertainty principle is not in play, and the QM frame contributes nothing beyond ordinary statistical thinking.

### 4. Category error: QM is a physical theory, not a problem-solving method

QM is *about* how matter behaves at small scales. It is not a generic framework for hard problems. "Translate any problem to QM" has the same shape as "translate any problem to fluid dynamics" or "translate any problem to plate tectonics" — answerable only by mathematical isomorphism, never by virtue of QM being QM.

When mathematical structure happens to be shared (e.g. random walks on graphs ↔ certain Schrödinger problems), use the math. Don't smuggle the "physics" wrapping; it adds metaphorical confidence without mathematical content.

### 5. Rigor laundering: borrowed vocabulary ≠ borrowed verification

QM is one of the most precisely verified theories in science (QED predictions agree with experiment to ten significant figures in some cases). When QM vocabulary is applied to a soft engineering question, the unstated rhetoric is "this analysis carries QM's rigor." It does not. The rigor of QM lives in *experimental verification of QM in physical systems*. Borrowed vocabulary inherits the words, not the experimental status.

This is exactly the failure mode the C.2 trial labelled *misleading*: the GR `entropy-surface` blueprint matched on the workflow step label "API SURFACE" and would have implied Hawking-entropy rigor where the underlying signal was a label string.

### 6. A framework that can describe anything constrains nothing

QM has a rich and evocative vocabulary: entanglement, decoherence, tunneling, uncertainty. For any pair of correlated software entities, one can choose to call them "entangled." For any uncertainty, one can choose to call it "Heisenberg uncertainty." If the choice is always available, the framework imposes no constraint — and a framework that forbids no observation predicts nothing (Popper). This is the falsifiability problem at the heart of analogical reasoning.

### 7. Level-of-description mismatch

Even within physics, QM is not used to describe ocean currents or weather. Navier-Stokes is the right *effective* theory at that level; QM machinery (superposition, entanglement) decoheres at macroscopic scales. Engineering problems live at human, semantic, social levels — further from QM's natural scale than weather is. Applying QM machinery to engineering is using a microscope to map a city.

### 8. The PhysicsConcern enumeration cannot scale to "any"

The adapter implements 12 concrete `PhysicsConcern` blueprints (catalogued in `2026-06-17-track-c-input-inventory.md`). To handle *arbitrary* problems would require either an unbounded enumeration or a closed taxonomy covering every engineering problem. The first is impossible; the second is empirically refuted by the three trial contexts that did not cleanly fit any of the twelve. "Any" cannot be honored by enumerated blueprints.

### 9. Metaphors decay; cognitive load compounds

Even where a specific mapping is useful at conception time, the metaphor degrades. New readers do not understand why coupling is "gravity" or why tests are "measurements"; they must learn both the original concept *and* the physics overlay. Maintenance cost grows over time without commensurate analytical benefit. This is a general property of decorative analogies: their explanatory return diminishes while their teaching cost is paid forever.

### 10. Computational irrelevance

QM became transformatively useful for technology when we built devices whose underlying physics is genuinely quantum: transistors, lasers, MRI, quantum computers. Software running on classical CPUs is not quantum at the semantic level; the silicon below is, but program meaning lives above the layer where quantum effects are observable. Translating code into QM does not recruit quantum hardware; it is a notational exercise. By contrast, formulating a problem so it *can run on a quantum computer* is a different (and well-defined) activity — it is the math, not the picture, that pays.

## Counter-cases (the steelman)

There *are* cases where structures borrowed from QM help:

- Quantum-inspired classical algorithms (tensor networks, MERA) work because the underlying *mathematics* — linear algebra over high-rank tensor spaces — is genuinely useful. The QM-derivation pathway is incidental; the math is the engine.
- Operator-algebra phrasings of probabilistic reasoning are sometimes clarifying, but the load is carried by linear algebra, not by physics interpretation.
- Decoherence as a metaphor for attention drift in distributed systems can be pedagogically clarifying, though not predictive.

The concession: if a specific engineering problem genuinely needs linear algebra over complex Hilbert spaces, use linear algebra over complex Hilbert spaces. Call the technique what it is. Resist labelling it "the QM picture" — that labelling promises more than the math delivers.

## Implications for the Track C re-evaluation trigger

The C.3 decision doc says the deprecation can be revisited when telemetry is enriched and richer engineering text is available for trial mappings. This conceptual analysis adds a stronger gate to that revisit:

- **Necessary but not sufficient:** richer telemetry might unblock the empirical trial, but a passing trial under richer telemetry does *not* establish a universal translation. It establishes one more concrete blueprint.
- **A revisit should be scoped to specific problem classes whose mathematical structure (linear superposition with operational interference, observables with genuine non-commutativity, Born-rule probabilities with operational meaning) maps cleanly to QM.** Concretely: an engineer should be able to point at a real engineering quantity that is *measured* differently when paired with another measurement — that is the precondition for QM machinery to do work.
- **Reject the universal framing on revisit.** Any future adapter should announce its scope as "this set of problem classes" and refuse inputs outside that scope. The original premise of "any" was a category error.

## Bottom line

The empirical answer from Track C is "deprecate, because it does not work." The conceptual answer is stronger: *it cannot work as a universal translator, because the proposed translation is not a structure-preserving map but a vocabulary substitution, and a vocabulary substitution does not inherit the rigor of the original theory*. The right unit of effort is not "fix the adapter so it covers more inputs" but "identify the small set of engineering problems whose mathematics actually matches QM machinery, and treat those — and only those — using that machinery, by its mathematical name."
